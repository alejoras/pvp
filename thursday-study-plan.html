1 · Strategic-question playbooks

For each: the likely question, the structure to answer with, and the story/framework to deploy. Rehearse the structure, not a script.

Q1. "How would you approach demand gen at HackerRank?" / "Where would you start?"

Structure: Diagnose before prescribe. "I'd resist jumping to tactics. First 90 days: (1) audit current growth loops and the full funnel end-to-end, find the biggest drop-offs; (2) talk to 10–15 customers + sales/SDRs on why accounts buy; (3) pick 2–3 high-leverage channels to test; (4) stand up measurement so every experiment has clean signal. By day 90: a growth thesis, 2–3 experiments live, baselines set."

Then connect to HackerRank: "Given the JD's ABM mandate and the enterprise eng-hiring ICP, I'd bias toward 1:Few ABM on high-fit accounts — the consumption model means more open reqs = bigger deals, so account selection is where the leverage is." (This echoes your take-home — shows continuity.)

Deploy: scientist framework + first-90-days. Don't over-claim certainty without data — that itself signals maturity.

Q2. "How do you decide which opportunities to pursue?" (prioritization)

Structure: "I prioritize on leverage, not noise. Early on, an Effort/Impact matrix; once there's data, RICE (reach, impact, confidence, effort). The discipline is killing the low-confidence bets fast, not running everything at once."

Proof: Brandly — paused the high-CAC LinkedIn lead-gen play pre-PMF rather than burn budget (story 3b), then re-invested once messaging resonated (3c).

Q3. "How do you use data to make decisions?"

Structure — the scientist: "(1) Gather data, find it if it doesn't exist; (2) hypothesize and test; (3) translate the result into a story to get buy-in — no decision lands without stakeholders."

Two proofs: the "Discovery" LinkedIn campaign — mined ad-engagement data to find Brandly's ICP when nobody knew who bought (3a); and the SUKU alpha miss — re-forecast off real conversion data, not engagement vanity, then fixed it with partnerships (4d).

Name your stack to show fluency: funnel diagnostics, UTM tracking, pivot tables, attribution, GA/Amplitude, plus self-taught SQL/Python/Tableau.

Q4. "Walk me through a program you built, optimized, and measured." (her explicit ask)

Best pick — Brandly integrated campaign (3c) or Partner Program (3d): both are full-funnel, multi-channel, measured, and map directly to the ABM 1:Many→1:Few→1:1 language in the JD.

Tell it as: situation → what you tested → what failed → what you changed → measured result. Lead with the number. For 3d: 25 contacted → 20 replied → 10 converted → 5 active partners, including Brandly's biggest client ever.

Backup — Polygon (4a) if she wants a big-splash creative campaign: 22M impressions, 47K installs, ~$5K budget.

Q5. "How would you build/scale ABM here?" (straight from the JD)

Structure: "ABM is a tiering discipline. 1:Many = broad intent-based plays to a segment; 1:Few = clustered accounts with shared pain and tailored creative; 1:1 = named-account, deeply personalized. The engine is account selection + signal + personalized touch, orchestrated with SDRs."

Proof: your take-home is a 1:Few/1:1 engine — two-lane account scoring that routes accounts to the right angle, verified contacts, personalized messaging, Tier-1 personalized landing pages. And Brandly's Partner Program was value-first ABM outbound before you had the tooling.

This is your strongest territory — you literally built the thing they're hiring for. Say so plainly.

4 · Take-home — 60-second recap + defense

She may use it as a talking point. Be able to summarize the system in 60 seconds, because system thinking is the evaluators' top criterion (a model that routes, not just ranks).

"I built a Clay-based outbound engine with two scoring lanes — AI-Innovator and Regulated-Scale — so a bank and an AI startup both qualify on their own strengths instead of one penalizing the other. It gates on fit, scores both lanes, routes each account to the email angle its winning lane implies, finds a verified eng + TA leader per account, and generates personalized messaging deterministically. Tier-1 accounts get a personalized AI-Readiness teardown page. Every prospect was POSTed and accepted."

If challenged "why two lanes?": "Because HackerRank's stronghold includes regulated enterprises — banks, insurers — that don't have high AI-role density. A single AI-weighted model would down-rank exactly the accounts they win. Two lanes fix that. It routes, it doesn't just rank."
